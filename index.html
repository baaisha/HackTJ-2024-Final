Basic statistics terms, Dotpots and Bar Graphs

Note 0.1 — Statistics is the science of learning from data. Data is always collected in real-world context.
Definition 0.2 — A variable is a characteristic of something that can be assigned a number or category.
Example 0.3. —Is the number 2.6 considered a variable? What is an example of one?

Walkthrough.

  1. Can it be assigned a number or meaning?
  2. What is something that you can measure about youself?

Definition 0.4 — The thing in which the category or number is assigned is called the observational unit. It is the objects or people that data is being collected from |observational unit| to |observational unit|; statistics would not be a thing if there was no variability
Definition 0.5 —
  • Quantitative variable measures a numerical characteristic
  • Categorical variable records a group designation; binary variables are categorical variables with only two possible categories
  • A research question often looks for patterns in different variables and looks for relationships between variables
    •
    Definition 0.6 — Dotplot: graphs a quantitative variable. Each quantitative data value is shown as a dot above its location on a number line
    1. Draw a horizontal number line with the variable name
    2. Scale the axis by looking at the minimum and maximum values
    3. Mark a dot above the location of horizontal axis for each observational unit
    Definition 0.7 — Bar graph: used to display data from a categorical variable or compare the size of different quantities (bars are not touching)
    1. Label both axes (categorical variable on the x-axis and quantitative data value on the y-axis)
    2. Draw bars that correspond to each variable and leave room in between each bar
    Note 0.8 — Dotplots and bar graphs are most illuminating when comparing the distribution of a variable between two or more groups
    Definition 0.9 —
    • Tendency: refers to the center of the distribution and where the data points are likely to fall.
    • Consistency: refers to the spread/range of the distribution (synonymous with spread, variability) and how likely the data points are to fall within a certain range
    • When comparing tendency, do not use the word greater or less than, simply compare the values
    Check your Understanding 1.0 —

    2. What type of variable is displayed in dot plots? What type of variable is displayed in bar graphs?
    3. When are dot plots and bar graphs most illuminating?
    4. What is consistency and tendency? How are they different?

All members of the population must have an equal chance of being sampled Random digit tables, calculators, and computers are good impersonal methods to select a random sample Using a random digits table: Decide the number of digits being looked for each time When under 10 items in the population, look for single digits (0-9) When under 100 items, look for 2 digits (00-99) When under 1000 items, look for 3 digits (000-999) Start at any point in the table and read the digits based on the number decided in step 1 E.g., if a population has 78 items, look for digits 01-78 (skip 00 and 79-99) Depending on the situation, you might skip repeat selections Show your work by circling/underlining digits being selected and crossing out digits being skipped Sample size refers to how many observational units are in a sample In an actual study, you should only take one sample from a given population If your sampling method is biased, taking a larger sample will not reduce the bias, but produce a more precise estimate that is still inaccurate to the population value If you are working with a truly random sample, it is reasonable to draw conclusions about a population from its sample The larger a sample, the more precise its statistics are Precision is how close the data points are to each other Sample size affects the precision of a sample Accuracy is how close the data points are to the accepted/predicted value Bias (or the lack of bias) affects the accuracy of a sample If you are not working with a random sample, what can you not do confidently? Not Precise Topic 5 (W9D1 KC) Anecdotal evidence: results of situations that come to mind easily, but have little to no use in scientific research. Statistics tries to avoid anecdotal evidence Experiment: study that treatment is actively imposed by the experimenter on subjects. Ideally, subjects are all identical other than the explanatory variable Random Assignment: preferred way of assigning subjects treatment to avoid confounding variables. Each subject has an equal chance of being assigned any treatment group. Study is called a randomized comparative experiment. It is often helpful to diagram an experiment using an experimental design diagram. Observational unit —> (arrows from observational to treatment indicate random assignment) —> Treatment 1 or Treatment 2 —> Compare results (mean or proportion) Comparison: Use a design to compare two or more treatments Random assignment: use chance to assign experimental units to treatments to create roughly equivalent groups of observational units by balancing the effects of other variables among treatment groups Control: Keeps other variables (excluding explanatory) the same for all groups, especially variables that are likely to affect response variable Replication: Uses enough experimental units in each group so that any difference in the effects of the treatments can be distinguished from chance difference between groups Blindness: experimental units are not aware of the experiment being done and the group that they are assigned to avoid outside influences Double blindness is when the experimenter also does not know the treatment assignments Random sampling aims to produce a sample that is representative of the population. Random sampling eliminates bias. Random assignment aims to produce treatment groups that are similar in all respects except for the treatments imposed.
